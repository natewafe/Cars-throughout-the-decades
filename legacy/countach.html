Lamborghini Countach LP400 Manufacturer: Lamborghini · Production: 1974–1990 · Country: Italy

Designed by Marcello Gandini for Bertone. The Countach introduced a radically angular, low-slung silhouette that permanently altered automotive design language. Its scissor doors, mid-engine layout, and extreme proportions made it the definitive supercar poster car of its era, and for two decades afterward.

375 Horsepower (LP400S)
5.2L V12 Engine
~5.6s 0–60 mph
183 Top Speed (mph)

Road & Track Road Test, February 1976 Primary Source · Period Review · Road & Track Magazine

The original print road test, photographed against the Italian countryside. Road & Track declared it "the fastest car we've ever tested." The spread captures contemporary critical reception, not the nostalgia of hindsight, but the raw shock of first encounter.

This Road and Track road test shows the original reaction to one of the first real performance tests of this car, the orange Countach on the track where they live-tested it and collected hard data. When manufacturers release their own numbers they can over or underestimate, so this was the first time a third-party source was actually able to dig into what this car could do. They were extremely surprised by how it handled, performed, and in every way felt ahead of its time. Road and Track recorded a 0-60 time of 6.8 seconds and observed a top speed near 163 mph, leading them to call it the Fastest Car We Have Ever Tested on the cover of their February 1976 issue. It was a car that looked like no other and truly defined the visual language of cars to come, and even looking back now, Lamborghini remade it and it still looks as seamless and cool as it did the day it first appeared.

Secondary Source

Perkins, Chris. "5 Things You Didn't Know About the Lamborghini Countach." The Drive, September 19, 2016.

The author argues the Countach was well ahead of its time, almost by accident, demonstrating this through prototypes and aerodynamic tests, making clear the car was made as spectacle rather than comfort. Provides context through schematics and specifics showing how far ahead of its era the car was, and the behind-the-scenes details of how it got there.
