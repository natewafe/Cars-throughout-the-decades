Countach

The car that made the future look like a wedge of steel.

Lamborghini Countach LP400 Manufacturer: Lamborghini · Production: 1974–1990 · Country: Italy

Designed by Marcello Gandini for Bertone. The Countach introduced a radically angular, low-slung silhouette that permanently altered automotive design language. Its scissor doors, mid-engine layout, and extreme proportions made it the definitive supercar poster car of its era — and for two decades afterward.

Road & Track Road Test — February 1976 Primary Source · Period Review · Road & Track Magazine

The original print road test, photographed against the Italian countryside. Road & Track declared it "the fastest car we've ever tested." The spread captures contemporary critical reception — not the nostalgia of hindsight, but the raw shock of first encounter.

Design Revolution

The Countach arrived as a provocation. Marcello Gandini's design for Bertone was so extreme that early prototypes were tested in wind tunnels just to confirm the body would generate meaningful downforce — which it did, almost by accident.

The LP400's original body was actually the least aerodynamically compromised version: clean flanks, minimal spoilers, a purity of form that the later widebody variants would sacrifice for grip. In that original shape, the Countach was a work of sculpture.

Perkins' 2016 retrospective in The Drive details how Lamborghini's engineers often had to reverse-engineer explanations for design decisions that Gandini had made purely for visual effect — and how many of those explanations turned out to be technically sound.

Cultural Footprint

By the late 1970s, the Countach had become the poster car — literally. It appeared on more bedroom walls than any other automobile in history, a cultural phenomenon that preceded the car's actual performance reputation.

The Road & Track road test of February 1976 is significant not just as data, but as rhetoric. The language used — superlatives, breathless astonishment — mirrors exactly how the car was understood: not as transportation, but as a statement about what human engineering could achieve.

Every wedge-shaped supercar since — the Ferrari Testarossa, the Diablo, the modern Huracán — owes its visual grammar to the Countach. The scissor door became Lamborghini's signature. The proportions became the genre's proportions.
